Works upon the Nature of Evil

Diary of Iniquity

being a commonplace book of the British canon — with a comparison of Augustine and Milton on whence evil comes

by Isabella Wu

❦

ENG-261 · British Literature · Prof. Steven Minas

The governing idea
Evil is not a substance but a privation — a hole where good ought to be.
Sir Augustine, in “The Green Chapel of the Soul”

Preface

Augustine said evil isn’t a substance — it’s a turning away from the good.

That line, written first in a diary about gossip, became the thread the whole semester pulled on. Each piece here answers a canonical text by speaking from inside its evil: a monster over a fresh kill, the weird sisters of the heath, a modern Faustus selling a machine that doesn’t work. The forms shift — confession, dramatic monologue, closet drama, morality play, critical essay — but the argument holds across them. The decisive theatre of moral evil is inward, and external voices cannot cause what is not already, in some embryonic way, desired.

The works are kept in the order they were written, the first week through the final essay.

I

Diary of Iniquity

by Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

A confession written straight: the small, repeated betrayal of telling someone else’s secret to feel close to a third person.

After Augustine & Milton · A reflective diary

I see my evil every day.

Not in the form of grand sins, but in the quiet, habitual betrayal of people I care about. It always starts the same: someone opens up to me. They tell me something private. It could be a fight they had with their partner, a failure that they're ashamed of, a comment they made that they regret. And later, when I'm with someone else, I bring it up explicitly or implicitly. I don't mean to. At least, that's what I tell myself. Sometimes, I'm just trying to connect to someone else by sharing secrets. I know gossiping is a sin. It's disloyalty. It's me choosing momentary attention over silent integrity.

Augustine said evil isn't a substance, it's a turning away from the good. I know what good is. The good is keeping my mouth shut. The good is to protecting someone else's story even when no one is watching. But I turn from it. Over and over again.

I want to stop. I tell myself I will. I make promises in my head like, Next time, I won't say anything. Next time, I'll catch myself. But the moment comes and I forget my promises. So I talk. Milton wrote that virtue must be exercised — that only by resisting temptation can we become strong. But sometimes I wonder if I've exercised vice so much that it's what comes naturally now. If this reflex to share and to spill makes me rotten at the core.

This is my iniquity. My daily, deliberate failure. My trial is not some big, dramatic temptation. My trial is in every conversation, where I have the chance to be better.

I'm writing this now, because I want to stop turning away from the good.

Appendix — Two accounts of evil

How Augustine and Milton each frame the same problem.
AspectAugustineMilton
View of evil Absence of good (privation); non-substance. Intertwined with good; necessary for virtue.
Free will Explains evil's origin — a turning away from God. Enables virtuous action through moral trial.
God's role Goodness only; no evil creation. Allows evil's existence for moral development.
Tone Philosophical, introspective, confessional. Rhetorical, poetic, dramatic.
Takeaway Avoid corruption by aligning with the good. Embrace trial to grow in virtue.

II

My Last Victim

Grendel speaks · by Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

Grendel stands over a fresh kill and explains himself, in the couplets of Browning’s jealous duke — pleading the doom that Cain’s blood fixed on him.

After Beowulf, by way of Browning · Dramatic monologue

Heorot Hall. Grendel stands over a freshly killed Dane, speaking to another warrior.

This is my last victim painted on the floor, Lying as if he breathed, but breathes no more. A wonder, sir! I call it craft, my art, Cain’s curse that sears unending in my heart. Will’t please you look? I set it so by will, Though truly will I lack, being fated still. You marvel how this ruin came to be? No choice, but doom, hath even spoken me. I am not twisted — sir, how could I bend Who never knew beginning nor shall end? To call me “corrupt” errs; for what am I But evil whole, which cannot fall nor die? Not courage struck him down, nor flashing blade, But ancient grief that Cain’s own hand had made. Too quick he laughed, too easily he sang; The harp, the boast, the mead-bench when it rang All pierced me like a thorn. He praised each sound As though my night could hold some equal ground. I roared, and still he smiled, that witless fool, Would weigh my curse against the hall’s bright rule! Who’d stoop to argue? Had I tongue to frame My will, or skill to chide his reckless game — Had I but said, “Your joy offends; desist; Your cheer is mockery, silence would be bliss” — Would he have yield? Nay! His will was free; Mine bounded by Cain, compelled to misery. So we were set: his laughter and my hate; And so I struck. He fell. That was his fate. There lies my work, as though he breathed again, Rise, sir; descend with me among the slain. Your lord may think his gold-gift binds this place, I claim it all, though none may call it grace. And yet — I think — if fate had shaped me so, And given me the strength that heroes know, I might have borne his lot, and sung his fame, And I lived not cursed, but blessed, with mortal name. If I were Beowulf, and he were I, The song might lift me up, not bid me die. But Cain was first, and I his eldest son; The doom is fixed, and what is writ is done. Nay, we’ll go down together, sir. But see — Yon great below would strive to master me. He dreams to bind the darkness with his hand; Let him be taught what none may understand.

P.S. — This monologue of Grendel is written in the style of Robert Browning's "My Last Duchess."

III

Grendel’s Confession on Evil

Grendel speaks · by Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

Across nine movements Grendel weighs his own evil against Augustine and Milton — and fears he is not corrupt but merely constrained, a curse he never chose.

After Beowulf, with Augustine & Milton · Blank verse in nine movements

A meditation in unrhymed iambic pentameter. Grendel speaks to no one, and to the reader.

I I am the mark, the shadow cast by Cain, Born in the swamps where memory festers dark. You name me monster, fiend, hell-spawn, brute, Yet I am but the shape of what was cursed. When brother slew his brother in the field. No innocence remained; the ground drank blood, And God pronounced the exile, sealed the fate, And all who sprang thereafter bore the mark. What choice had I, who opened first my eyes Upon a world already scorched by wrath? What freedom claims the son of such a stain? You call me evil, yet what is that word? A brand, a boundary, spoken to define The outcast that your laws cannot forgive. II Augustine says evil is not a thing, But lack, a privation, absence of the good. If so, then I am hollowed from my birth, Not twisted, not corrupted, but complete In emptiness — a vessel never filled. A harp may sound when struck by human hands; In me there is no chord for joy to pluck, No string to vibrate into sweeter tones. The hall resounds with music, feast, and light, And still I gnash my teeth and feel a wound Deeper than spears can pierce. That wound is want, The hunger of a soul that lacks the good. What blame to thirst when fountains never flow? What blame to hunger when no bread is near? So if I strike, it is the want that strikes, The void that claws, the famine that devours. III Milton has said of Satan in his fall That “evil, be thou my good,” became the creed. In him I hear a kinship, though no choice. For Satan chose defiance, raised his head Against the throne, and said his famous words: “Better to reign in Hell than serve in Heaven.” But I, poor beast, cannot proclaim such pride. I do not reign. I do not serve. I rot. My kingdom is the fen, the dripping mere, My court the fog, my courtiers the reeds. If Satan sinned by will, then I by fate; If he by envy, I by inheritance. He built a hell of mind, heroic still; I only dwell in mire, mute with shame. Yet both of us are mirrors of one truth: That evil has its roots in separation. He broke from God; I never could belong. He lost the light; I never saw it blaze. IV Augustine speaks of will enslaved by sin, The twisting of desire toward the dust. But tell me, priest, what will was ever mine? Did I select the blood that filled my veins? Did I select the curse that sealed my brow? If evil is corruption of the good, Then where the germ of good in me to spoil? The poet says: I am the son of Cain, Outlaw to men, outcast from God’s own face. So when I creep at night to tear the flesh, I merely walk the road already drawn. Would you condemn the stream for running down, Or blame the fire for burning when it leaps? Then why condemn the issue of a curse, Who follows only what was writ at birth? V And yet, I hear the laughter in the hall, The harp, the poet singing men’s first dawn, The light that clothed the world when God declared “Let there be” and my entrails twist with grief. If I were free, perhaps I might rejoice, Lift up my hand, and share the wine and song. But always, like a shadow at the gate, I linger, barred and bitterness returns. They call their hall Heorot, the heart of men; My heart is fen, where no fire warms the mud. Perhaps this too is evil, envy’s breath. Augustine named it rightly: disordered love. I love the sound, but not for what it is; I love it only as what I am denied. Thus love itself is poisoned into hate. VI And yet, imagine, if the lots were changed, If Grendel had been born in Geatland’s court, A warrior praised for valor in the mead, If I were he, and Beowulf were I. Would Beowulf then stalk the moors by night, Exiled, chained to curse and Cain’s descent? And would men sing of Grendel’s generous hand, His gift of rings, his justice in the hall? If fate had clothed me in a different flesh, Would I not bear the marks of nobility? This thought consumes me more than hunger’s bite: Not that I am, but that I might have been. The curse is not corruption but constraint. I cannot choose, and therefore cannot change. VII Milton’s fiend at least proclaims his “no” With grandeur, making Hell itself a throne. I have no throne, save slime and carrion stench. Where he has rhetoric, I have only roar; Where he has legions, I have solitude. And yet, within my solitude I brood On him, on Adam, Eve, on Abel slain. And often I have wondered: If the fall Of one man’s hand could ripple down through time, Then all my malice merely echoes his. I am not evil, I am aftermath, The echo of a crime the world will not heal. Augustine says that evil has no life; It parasitizes goodness like a worm. If so, then I am parasite of song: I hate the harp because I cannot sing. I tear the body because I cannot love. VIII Beowulf comes. I hear his tread below. He thinks himself the light against my dark, The hero sent by providence to save. And maybe so. If Providence is real, Then I am proof of Providence reversed: That God ordains not only joy, but curse. Perhaps my death will make his glory shine, As Satan’s fall made Christ more radiant still. But think: is Providence a net of steel? Then even heroes march by fated thread. And if so, what distinction lies between The monster and the man? If both are cast As players in a tale already writ, Why damn the one and praise the other so? Why crown with laurel him, and me with chain, When neither wrote the script that we enact? IX Yet now I smell the iron of the swords, The mead upon his breath, the heat of flesh. He waits to grasp me, eager for my fall. And I, compelled, will hurl myself again Against the light, though knowing I must fail. For evil is not victory but wound, Not triumph but the endless repetition Of grief. Augustine was right: the soul Made for the good will gnaw itself in vain. And Milton too was right: to name the loss, To call it kingdom though it be a pit. And I, Grendel, stand between the two, A beast who cannot choose, yet still can weep. So write my name in margins of your books, Call me a fiend, a shadow, Cain’s descent. But know that in my howling you may hear A mirror of your own unquiet thoughts: That evil is a hunger never filled, A wound that cannot heal, a song unlearned, A shadow not of choice but of despair.

Artist Statement

I chose to write Grendel’s Confession as a blank-verse dramatic monologue because this form gives the silent antagonist a voice that is both intimate and profound. I mimicked the style of Robert Browning’s “My Last Duchess” to write this dramatic monologue. Poem, as a style, allows a single speaker to reveal his motives and psychology while the audience listens in judgement. Grendel is never granted such interiority in the Old English poem; he exists only as a monster described by others. By letting him speak directly, I explore the tension between how society names evil and how the supposedly evil creature understands himself.

Blank verse as unrhymed iambic pentameter links Grendel’s soliloquy to the epics. Shakespeare used this meter for tragedy and Milton chose it for Paradise Lost, the text that shapes my understanding of Satan and free will. Its natural rhythm lets complex theological ideas unfold in a conversational way. I avoided rhyme so the meditation on sin and fate could move freely without the sing-song quality of closed couplets.

In general, poem offers a more human touch to the discussion of evil than an analytical essay could provide. Rather than presenting arguments in a scholarly style, poem stages an interior dialogue where thought and feeling merge. Grendel is no longer a flat emblem of monstrosity but a living voice. He questions fate, God and the meaning of sin. In letting him speak, I invite readers to share not only ideas but emotions: grief, envy, hunger for light. This is what I want to express with my work. I’m hoping to seek understanding through lived experience instead of abstract reasoning.

For these reasons I chose poetry rather than essay. Poetry holds argument and emotion together, creating a space where philosophy becomes personal and where a monster can sound human.

IV

The Green Chapel of the Soul

A play in one evening · by Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

A bishop and a knight meet in a ruined chapel and argue whether a scar is a shame or a sacrament.

After Sir Gawain & Augustine · Closet drama in five scenes

Characters.  Sir Augustine — Bishop of Hippo imagined as a knightly theologian, carrying the book of his Confession like a shield.  Sir Gawain — Knight of the Round Table, still wearing the green sash of his trial.  An attendant.

Setting. A ruined chapel deep in a winter forest. Moonlight falls through a broken rose window. A small fire burns at center stage. The sound of a distant bell.

Scene I — The Meeting

Sir Gawain enters, cloak dusted with snow. Sir Augustine is already seated by the fire, reading.

Gawain

Good evening, venerable knight. I had thought this chapel empty.

Augustine

No house of prayer is ever empty, friend. The Word fills all things. Sit and warm yourself.

Gawain

(He sits.) I know you by repute: Augustine, bishop and seeker of God. Yet you bear a sword.

Augustine

A courtesy to the age we meet in. One never knows what dragons the heart may hide.

Scene II — On Sin and Trial

Gawain

I come from the Green Chapel, where I bent my neck to the axe. I flinched, I confessed, and I live, scarred by a nick no armour can hide.

Augustine

You call that scar a shame. I call it a sacrament. Sin is a wound, but the wound can teach. In my youth I stole pears not for hunger but for love of theft. The fruit rotted, yet the memory ripened into confession.

Gawain

Then you too have worn the green girdle of guilt.

Augustine

Indeed. And I learned that evil is not a substance but a privation — a hole where good ought to be. The heart turns from the Light, and darkness follows as shadow follows body.

Gawain

Yet the Green Knight was no shadow. His axe was real, his laughter like thunder. Evil seemed present, not absent.

Augustine

Presence can be the mask of absence. Your temper was an instrument of providence. Through him God revealed your pride and your fear.

Scene III — On Free Will and Grace

Gawain

But what of choice? I chose to wear the girdle to save my life. Was I free, or driven by dread?

Augustine

The will is free enough to fall, but too frail to rise without grace. You chose; God sustained. Even your fear became the narrow gate through which mercy entered.

Gawain

So courage lies not in flawless deed but in the confession after?

Augustine

Just so. The bravest act is to kneel.

Scene IV — On the Heroic Heart

Gawain

I sought honour in arms. You sought God in thought. Are we then brothers?

Augustine

Brothers indeed. The knight fights dragons of flesh and steel. The bishop fights dragons of memory and desire. Both battles end in surrender to the same King.

Gawain

Then my scar is no disgrace.

Augustine

It is your heraldry of grace.

Scene V — Benediction

The chapel bells grow louder.

Gawain

The night wanes. Will you ride with me to Camelot?

Augustine

My road lies inward, yet our paths are one. Go, and tell your Round Table that the truest chivalry is humble repentance.

Gawain

And you, sir bishop-knight, tell your scholars that the truest wisdom wears a sword only to guard the heart.

Moonlight brightens; the bells fade.

V

Dr. Holmes

A Modern Morality Parody · by Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

Marlowe’s Doctor Faustus recast at Theranos, with Sunny Balwani as the tempter and the press as the chorus.

After Marlowe’s Doctor Faustus · A morality play in three scenes

The persons of the play, against their originals.

Dr. Holmes (Faustus) — Elizabeth Holmes, a charismatic young entrepreneur; a genius for persuasion and storytelling with profound moral deficiency. Like Faustus, she seeks "omnipotence" not in black magic but in the impossible medical invention that brings her fame and money.

Sunny (Mephistophilis) — Sunny Balwani, drawn to Holmes by her "attractively sinful frame of mind," offering the modern equivalent of "magic": his business experience lets her bypass the hard ethical work of science for the reward of being a celebrated billionaire.

The Media (the Chorus) — the disembodied voice of public opinion and judgement.  The Old Man — her former professor at Stanford, who once mentored her and later exposed the fraud.  Robin & Dick — two entry-level lab employees.  A Patient.

Scene I — The Bargain of Ambition

Dr. Holmes sits alone, surrounded by stacks of failed test reports. She is dressed immaculately, but her frustration is palpable.

Dr. Holmes

I have my degrees, I have my patents, but what good are they? My ideas are too grand for this world. I want to revolutionize health care, to see all the data from a single drop of blood. But the machine — they fail! This ambition, once pure, grows "glutted now with learning's golden gifts." Why must the pursuit of "omnipotence" be bound by mere science? "Divinity, adieu!" — if truth be so restricting.

Sunny enters, sleekly dressed, observing her with calculating calm.

Sunny

You seek a shortcut, a way to make your vision a reality, no matter the cost. I see in you the mind that is "too subtle" for the "petty wits" who call your work impossible.

Dr. Holmes

(looks up) Who are you?

Sunny

I am here to offer a deal. The investor you need, the fame you crave, the technology you've dreamed of. All can be yours. We will move faster than the cautious world.

Dr. Holmes

What do I give in return?

Sunny

Nothing but… your vision. Your truth. You will lie to investors, claiming possibility where only failure resides. You will promise what is not yet possible. And for that, I will give you the reality of success. You just need to use the prerecorded test results that guarantee a positive show.

Dr. Holmes hesitates. The temptation is absolute. She picks up a pen and signs a document with a trembling hand, using her own blood.

Dr. Holmes

(To herself) My senses are deceiv'd, here's nothing writ. This is not a sin; it is innovation. The end justifies the means.

Sunny

(A cold smile) "Ay, think so still, till experience change thy mind."

A sudden, bright light illuminates her face, projecting a giant image of her on the cover of a magazine. Sunny nods. Scene ends.

Scene II — The Frivolous Power

A year passes. Dr. Holmes is on a small stage, giving a charismatic presentation next to "the Edison" machine. The Media speaks over her, their tone shifting from praise to doubt.

Media

She has reached the pinnacle of her craft. The media hails her as a visionary. Her ambition swells, yet her rewards are "empty," and her deeds approach the "frivolous."

Dr. Holmes points at the machine. Robin and Dick enter, holding a box of pizza.

Robin

(To Dick) Does she even know our machine uses a competitor's analyzer? She wastes her brain on delusion, making us look like fools.

Dick

(To Robin) She must be "glutted with conceit of this" vision. All this fame is built on a lie. "All the crime for nothing," I tell you.

A sickly-looking Patient approaches Dr. Holmes, holding a complex lab form.

Patient

Dr. Holmes, I've come from a great distance. I seek an explanation of my illness. I heard that the Edison machine has the ultimate power to know all things from one drop.

Dr. Holmes

(smiling widely) Of course. We will resolve "all ambiguities." We are changing the world.

The Old Man, her former professor, appears at the edge of the stage, holding papers that look like the due-diligence report.

Old Man

(Quietly, voice laced with disappointment) O gentle Dr. Holmes, leave this damned art. You have abandoned science for fame. "No mortal can express the pains of hell" that await you, for the lie hardens the heart.

Dr. Holmes

(Her smile falters. She gestures to the machine.) This technology is destined to "threat the stars with her aspiring top."

Sunny

(Stepping forward, putting a hand on Dr. Holmes's shoulder) Pay the old fool no mind. He is only here to "dissuade" you. We move forward. His advice is worthless in the market.

Scene III — The Reckoning and Despair

The lab is dark, the Edison machine covered in a black sheet. The Chorus speaks, solemn.

The Media

The allotted years of the contract have run their course. The corporate structure has dissolved, and the promises are shattered. The deceit is utterly unveiled for all to witness — the Wall Street Journal article has brought forth the truth.

Dr. Holmes stands utterly alone. Sunny materializes behind her, his face showing only the finality of a collector.

Sunny

(No longer tempting, simply stating the fixed consequence) Your term is complete; the hour has arrived, and the debt must be paid. Prepare now to face the fate of the eternally lost.

Dr. Holmes

(In a desperate whisper) My spirit is unbreakable, yet I cannot now turn back. I am entirely consumed by utter hopelessness. What did I truly gain from this contract? An empty vessel. The prospect of imprisonment. All of my transgressions, for a brief moment of glory, and now… absolutely nothing.

The Old Man, her former professor, enters, holding the newspaper that detailed the fraud.

Old Man

(His voice filled with conviction and disappointment) You are utterly condemned! Give in to your hopelessness! Stop! I see a glimmer of divine mercy hovering above you. Though you have deeply erred, your soul remains capable of redemption — if only sin does not entirely consume your nature.

Dr. Holmes

(Screaming at Sunny, placing the blame externally) You seductive betrayer! It was your enticing influence that stole my life.

Sunny

(shrugging) I admit to having facilitated your desire, and I rejoice in the final conclusion. Those who revel in the petty pleasure of this life are destined to endure deep sorrow in the next.

Dr. Holmes collapses in utter defeat. The Media delivers the final word.

The Media

Her life's time of freedom is now measured, and eternal ruin awaits. She who sought to perform deeds beyond the reach of human authority is now a miserable, doomed wretch. The promising life that might have flourished has been tragically severed.

The lights slowly fade on Dr. Holmes, leaving only the Edison machine.  End.

Artist Statement

My work, Dr. Holmes, is inspired by Marlowe's Doctor Faustus to explore ambition, deception and moral decay in a contemporary context. I aim to show that the Faustian bargain is not just a relic of literature, but a recurring pattern in human experience.

The journey of both Faustus and Holmes begins with an insatiable ambition that outstrips their current reality. Faustus, a brilliant scholar, grows "glutted with learning's golden gifts" and rejects traditional disciplines in favor of black magic to gain "a world of profit and delight, of power, of honor, of omnipotence." His aspirations are boundless, aiming to become "a demi-god." Similarly, Elizabeth Holmes, a young and ambitious entrepreneur, sought to revolutionize healthcare with a new technology for blood testing. Like Faustus, she desired to achieve something extraordinary, to prove that her vision — initially dismissed by experts as impossible — could become a reality. She too wanted power and recognition, seeking to be praised as a successful businesswoman.

Their pursuit of these visions led both to a reliance on deception and illusions. Faustus uses his powers for "frivolous purposes" like pranking the Pope and making a horse turn into a heap of straw. His immense power is used for petty and meaningless acts, serving only to maintain his facade of greatness. Holmes's actions mirror this, as she used "smoke and mirrors" to sell a fraudulent vision, lying to investors and claiming her technology worked when it did not. She put on a show for investors and the public, creating a grand delusion that was, in the end, as empty as Faustus' magic tricks.

The central parallel between their stories is the idea of "all the crime for nothing." Faustus's deal with the devil grants him twenty-four years of power, but his reward is unfulfilling. His life, after selling his soul, is full of petty pranks. In the end, his "greatest accomplishments are often trivial and disappointing." He ultimately gains no lasting knowledge or sense of purpose. Holmes's story follows a similar trajectory. Her fraudulent scheme brought her fame and wealth, but like Faustus's short-lived power, it was fleeting and ultimately futile. The company, Theranos, failed, and she was convicted of multiple counts of wire fraud and conspiracy. Her ambition led not to glory, but to her public downfall and a prison sentence. The years of lying and manipulation were all for nothing, just as Faustus's damnation was earned through a series of "disappointing" acts.

In this way, their stories serve as timeless cautionary tales. They remind us that trading honesty for ambition and integrity for power — whether for a moment of glory or a lifetime of deceit — can lead to eternal despair.

VI

The Witches’ Sonnets

The three witches · by Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

Each witch takes a sonnet, glossing her own prophecy against the very lines of the play it foretells.

Week 6 · After Macbeth · Three sonnets, with annotations

I · First Witch I call the storm, the thunder answers me,1 And murmur secrets through the curling smoke; A crown awaits,2 yet not for him to see The chain of blood that tightens like a yoke. I show the man what he would gladly take, A throne that glitters brighter than the sun; Yet all I promise bends his soul to break, For once begun, his work is never done.3 A dagger dances,4 hanging in the air, He grasps at shadows, swears he feels its steel; What fairer snare than promises most fair, What subtler snare than what his thoughts conceal? I tempt with crowns, but leave the cost unshown. He takes the bait, and makes the guilt his own. II · Second Witch I laugh to see the noble falter so, A plaything caught within our woven thread;5 He grasps at crowns, but little does he know That what he seizes leads him to the dead. Banquo will smile,6 though buried in the ground, His line shall stretch until the end of time; And still Macbeth will wear his borrowed crown,7 A thief of fate who cannot mask his crime. We never lie,8 yet truth can twist and bite, Our riddles bind him tighter than a chain; He sees the forest march9 against his might, And shudders when the womb denies his reign.10 I cackled loud, for all as it seems: A truth half-told shall rot his golden dreams. III · Third Witch I speak of ends, of dust, of death’s design,11 The final hush that swallows king and knave; What boots ambition, plotting, crowns or wine, When all must sink into the selfsame grave? The lady walks with candle in her hand,12 Her murmured cries13 the price of bloody gain; The tyrant grips a sword he cannot stand, A hollow king, enthroned in endless pain. VII

When the Will Goes Wrong

Macbeth, Frankenstein, and the Metaphysics of Moral Collapse · by Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

Macbeth’s daggers and Frankenstein’s Creature as one structure — a will that turns from the good, and an intellect that loses its grip on the real.

Week 7 · Macbeth & Frankenstein · Critical essay

Although Frankenstein appears later in class, I’ve already read it, and doing so after Macbeth illuminated a striking continuity between the two works; one that is not merely literary but philosophical. Both texts present what we might call a drama of disordered powers: a rational agent whose faculties cease to function according to the ends for which they are properly suited. The result, in each case, is a kind of moral and metaphysical collapse. The tragedy in Shakespeare and the Gothic horror in Shelley differ in surface form, yet they enact the same underlying structure: a will that turns toward the wrong good, and an intellect that subsequently loses clarity, stability, and truth.

In Macbeth, the witches’ prophecy is not an alien intrusion but a revelation of a possibility already lurking within Macbeth’s modal landscape. The prophecy merely makes manifest what could be true in some nearby possible world: Macbeth as king. But the moment he assents to that possibility, he accepts it not merely as conceivable but as desirable. His will diverges from its proper orientation. This is the first misstep. The subsequent murders and tyrannies are not arbitrary choices but the logical unfolding of a will already bent. Where the will deviates, the intellect soon falters. Macbeth begins to perceive what is not there: daggers suspended in the air, ghosts visible only to him. These hallucinations are not supernatural gimmicks; they are the epistemic consequences of a moral deviation. When the will loses its rightful aim, cognitive faculties fail to function properly; the mind becomes fertile ground for self-deception, fear and false perception. His tragedy is thus not merely psychological but metaphysical. The very conditions under which he interprets reality have become corrupted.

Frankenstein undergoes a parallel deviation, but with a crucial distinction. His temptation is not political ambition but the aspiration to transcend creaturely limits. In successfully animating lifeless matter, he performs an act with vast moral implications. And yet, the very moment he succeeds, his will recoils. He refuses to acknowledge the moral obligations generated by his own creative act. This refusal constitutes the central catastrophe of the novel. It represents not a violation of a social norm but a deeper metaphysical failure: a repudiation of the relationship between creator and created. And just as Macbeth’s disordered will manifests inwardly in visions, Victor’s manifests outwardly in the Creature, an embodied form of his dereliction. What Shakespeare internalizes, Shelley externalizes. The Gothic renders visible the same truth that tragedy dramatizes within the soul.

Both narratives thus illustrate a single philosophical principle: when the will becomes misaligned — when it seeks power divorced from the good, or creation divorced from responsibility — the entire noetic and moral structure of the self disintegrates. In Macbeth’s case, the corruption takes the form of psychological fragmentation; his very capacity to apprehend the world becomes unreliable. In Victor’s case, the corruption reshapes external reality; the Creature becomes the empirical consequence of a misdirected will, pursuing Victor in the same way Macbeth’s guilt relentlessly pursues him. In such stories, evil does not burst in from outside; it arises from a failure of proper function within the agent’s moral architecture.

For this reason, Macbeth can be understood as a conceptual predecessor to the Gothic vision that Frankenstein later perfects. Shakespeare delineates the inward mechanics: how a disordered will fractures the cognitive faculties and reconfigures one’s moral perception. Shelley takes that internal collapse and grants it physical form, giving shape and agency to the consequences of Victor’s abdication. The Gothic does not invent new moral insights; it manifests, with brutal clarity, the same metaphysical pattern tragedy has long articulated.

Thus, the two texts, though centuries apart, converge on one insight: moral ruin originates not in circumstances but in the will’s orientation. Macbeth is destroyed by the act he freely chooses; Victor is destroyed by the responsibility he refuses to choose. One collapses inward, the other outward, but both reveal the same truth: evil is not merely an action performed but a distortion of the soul’s teleology. Once the will fastens onto an improper good — ambition without virtue, mastery without mercy — the unraveling of the self becomes not only likely but inevitable. Reality itself, internal or external, begins to warp around that deviation.

This is why reading Macbeth first made Frankenstein so philosophically legible. The tragedy offered the conceptual map; the Gothic supplied the embodiment. Together they portray the same metaphysical law: that the integrity of the world, whether the inner world of perception or the outer world of creation, depends on the upright orientation of the will. When that orientation is lost, monsters appear. Sometimes, as with Macbeth, they rise within the mind. Sometimes, as with Victor, they rise from the laboratory table. But in both cases, the origin is the same: a rational agent who has ceased to love the good as he ought.

VIII

Vision, Virtue, and the Cost of Desire

The Lady of Shalott and Milton’s Comus · by Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

Two confined women, two kinds of vision: Milton’s Lady reasons her way out, Tennyson’s looks once at the world and dies of it.

Week 8 · Comus & The Lady of Shalott · Comparative essay

Both The Lady of Shalott by Tennyson and Comus by Milton explore what it means to live virtuously in a world of temptation and illusion. Both poems center on a confined woman: Milton’s Lady in the enchanted chair of Comus, Tennyson’s Lady in her tower. Each character confronts the boundaries between purity and experience, mind and body, self and world. Yet, while Milton’s heroine triumphs, Tennyson’s Lady falls through the pursuit of vision. The two poets frame virtue differently: Milton as the harmony of will and reason under divine order, Tennyson as the tragic longing for meaning in an age of doubt.

Isolation and the Mirror of the Mind

Milton’s Lady and Tennyson’s Lady both inhabit worlds of symbolic isolation. In Comus, the Lady wanders into the dark wood, separated from her brothers and surrounded by illusion. In The Lady of Shalott, the tower itself becomes a psychological space of separation. She is cursed to “weave the mirror’s magic sights,” seeing life only as reflection, never direct experience. The mirror becomes an emblem of Victorian consciousness — mediated, reflective and self-aware.

While Milton’s Lady is tested by external temptation, Tennyson’s Lady is undone by internal desire. Her yearning to look directly upon reality is embodied in the figure of Lancelot, which leads to the shattering of the mirror and the unraveling of her art. “The mirror crack’d from side to side,” Tennyson writes, a moment both liberating and fatal. Her quest for vision echoes Eve’s temptation in Paradise Lost, a desire to see, to know, to experience the fullness of the world.

Virtue and Temptation

For Milton, chastity is not repression but reasoned mastery of the self. The Lady in Comus resists Comus’s persuasive hedonism by grounding her virtue in the “sacred light of reason.” Her moral triumph is intellectual: she recognizes that freedom without order becomes slavery to appetite. Tennyson, however, reimagines chastity as tragic isolation. The Lady of Shalott is not tempted by lust but by life itself, by the beauty of Camelot and the vibrancy of human contact. Her death is not punishment but consequence: to see truth directly in a fallen world is to perish.

Thus, Milton’s Lady survives temptation through divine reason, while Tennyson’s Lady succumbs through aesthetic longing. Both embody the limits of human vision, but where Milton offers redemption through faith and grace, Tennyson offers only beauty and melancholy.

Art, Desire and Fall

In both poems, the female figure also represents the artist. Milton’s Lady defends the soul’s purity amid illusion; Tennyson’s Lady weaves illusions into art. Her loom parallels the poet’s act of creation, mediated by reflection rather than direct life. When she turns toward reality, the art collapses. The poem thus dramatizes a Victorian anxiety about art and morality. Can beauty exist without falsehood?

Milton’s art is a moral instruction — poetry as moral clarity. Tennyson’s poem is elegiac — poetry as mourning for a lost harmony. The Lady of Shalott’s floating body, “dead-pale between the houses high,” becomes an image of art’s sacrifice, a vision seen too clearly.

Conclusion

Both Comus and The Lady of Shalott explore the fragile line between vision and virtue. Milton’s Lady resists illusion through faith in divine order; Tennyson’s Lady breaks illusion through an act of self-assertion that ends in death. One affirms the moral sovereignty of reason; the other laments the isolation of consciousness.

Tennyson’s Lady lives in a post-Miltonic world, a world where the divine harmony Milton assumed has fractured into mirrors, reflections, and longing. Her tragedy is not sin but separation: the price of desiring truth in an age that no longer guarantees it.

IX

On Paradise Lost

Two reflections · by Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

Two readings of Milton’s Satan and the Fall — why his freedom is really pride, and why the forbidden fruit now looks like a phone.

Weeks 9–11 · Milton’s Paradise Lost · Personal reflection

I · On Satan, and the Tears of Adam

I found myself thinking a lot about Milton’s Satan. I used to think of him simply as the villain of the story, but after class discussion and reading some Romanticism interpretations (especially Blake and Shelley), I started to see how complicated he actually is. The early books of the poem almost trick us into admiring him. His speeches are grand and defiant. He sounds like a revolutionary leader or a tragic hero. “Better to reign in Hell than serve in Heaven” (I.263) feels so powerful at first, but the more I read, the more I realized how Milton builds irony into every part of that power. The freedom Satan claims is actually a kind of slavery to his own pride.

What struck me most was how modern this feels. Satan’s charisma reminds one of the way certain public figures or influencers can use confidence and language to gain followers, even when their motives are selfish. In that sense, Milton’s Satan is not just a theological figure, he’s psychological. His rebellion starts as idealism but turns into ego. By the time he reaches Eve, he’s no longer the bold angel defying injustice. He’s manipulative, resentful, and desperate to make someone else fall with him. When Milton turns him into a serpent, it feels symbolic of the way ego collapses in on itself, from something glorious and high to something crawling and deceptive.

It’s also interesting how Milton’s critique of Satan connects to our own time. The serpent’s whisper promising “knowledge” and “freedom” sounds a lot like the promises of technology or social media today, knowledge without wisdom, autonomy without accountability. It makes me think Milton was, in a strange way, predicting a modern version of moral crisis. What happens when rebellion becomes performance, or when charisma replaces truth.

The next part of the poem, on Adam and Eve’s repentance and their version of redemption, hits me differently. Milton doesn’t end the story in despair. Even though the Fall brings suffering and exile, the poem insists that moral growth only happens through trials. When Adam and Eve leave Eden, hand in hand, I felt it wasn’t just tragic but humane. Their “natural tears they dropped, but wiped them soon” (XII.645) is one of the most moving lines I’ve ever read. It’s about the courage to keep living after disappointment, to turn loss into learning.

The ending of Paradise Lost, where Adam discovers “the paradise within thee, happier far” (XII.587), also feels personal. It suggests that goodness, or paradise, isn’t a place we can lose. It’s something we have to rebuild within ourselves. That idea connects to the moral lessons in earlier readings from Beowulf and Sir Gawain and the Green Knight, but Milton makes it more inward and modern. Instead of physical quests or external monsters, the real battle is within the self, between pride and humility, ego and conscience.

Overall, I think Milton’s Paradise Lost is not only about theology but about the psychology of moral growth. It’s about how easily we confuse rebellion with freedom, and how redemption isn’t about going back to perfection but moving forward with awareness. It’s strange, but I feel like I understand myself a little bit better after reading it. The poem made me realize that the hardest battles, like Satan’s and Adam’s, are not fought with swords or angels but in the will.

II · The Forbidden Fruit in Our Own Time

When I first read Milton’s Paradise Lost, I expected an old religious poem filled with difficult language and distant theology. But the more I read, the more I realized how deeply modern it feels. Beneath its grand scale and battles lies something we still face today: the conflict between freedom and obedience, curiosity and restraint, pride and love.

Paradise Lost isn’t just about Adam and Eve or Satan. It’s about us. It speaks to the modern condition: our endless pursuit of knowledge, our fear of limits, and our longing for meaning in a world that often feels fallen. Even though Milton wrote in the seventeenth century, his poem captures something timeless about the human soul. It shows that moral growth begins with imperfection, that knowledge can both elevate and destroy. The true freedom comes only with humility. In the world of AI, social media and instant gratification, Paradise Lost feels less like a story from the past and more like a mirror held up to the present.

When I think of Eve’s temptation, I sometimes wonder what “forbidden fruit” looks like in our own time. For me, it’s not an apple, it’s technology. Tools like ChatGPT offer what Eve was promised: quick and effortless knowledge. They can answer almost any question, summarize entire books, and even sound “wise” in their advice. Using them feels powerful. But Milton’s warning echoes here too. Just as Eve’s curiosity led her to overstep, our generation faces the same temptation to use technology without critical thinking and independent reflection. I often had to force myself not to use ChatGPT by studying with another classmate, so that we were under the risk of reporting each other. But still, sometimes I resort to AI for things I’ve struggled with for a very long time. When I do that, I realize how easy it is to lose the slower but authentic learning process to using AI. Paradise Lost helps me see that the danger is not in the tool itself, but in the way we use it. Knowledge without humility becomes pride; information without consideration becomes noise.

Milton would probably say that ChatGPT is neither angel nor devil. It’s the fruit of our own making. Like Eve, we can eat from it wisely, using it to learn and grow, or we can consume it carelessly and forget what thinking really means. Paradise Lost doesn’t tell us to reject knowledge, it teaches us to hold it with reverence.

After the Fall, Milton’s focus shifts from disobedience to love. Adam and Eve’s story becomes less about punishment and more about renewal. When Adam says, “How can I live without thee?” his words are full of heartbreak and devotion. He chooses to share Eve’s fate, not because it’s rational, but because it’s human. Out of love, he accepts imperfection. That moment always strikes me as Milton’s greatest act of mercy. The world may have fallen, but love survives the Fall. In the end, Adam and Eve are not destroyed but transformed.

Their love evolves from innocence to understanding. They learned that to love someone is not to preserve perfection but to forgive imperfection. In modern life, where relationships are often measured by convenience, Milton’s message feels radical: love means walking together even after failure. Their final act, walking hand in hand out of Eden, shows that hope begins not in perfection, but in persistence.

Despite all its darkness, Paradise Lost is not a hopeless poem. In fact, its ending is quietly triumphant. After their expulsion, Adam and Eve weep, but they also pray, learn and forgive. They discover that failure is not final. Milton’s closing image of the two walking hand in hand into an unknown world is one of the most hopeful in literature. “The world was all before them,” he writes, and in those words, the reader feels both the weight of loss and the possibility of renewal. That image stays with me whenever I feel uncertain about the future. It reminds me that we are all, in some way, exiles from paradise, trying to build meaning in an imperfect world. Milton’s message isn’t that we should avoid falling, but that we should learn from our faults. Redemption, he suggests, begins with honesty and humility. To admit error is not weakness, it’s the first act of moral courage.

For me, Paradise Lost feels less like a story about the beginning of sin than a story about the beginning of conscience. It captures what it means to be human, which includes the capability of falling but also rising again. Milton wrote about Heaven and Hell, but what he really described was the space in between — the human heart.

A Preface to Paradise Lost

On C. S. Lewis · by Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

Reading Lewis on how to read Milton: judge the cathedral as a cathedral, and watch Satan degrade from hero to snake.

Week 12 · C. S. Lewis, A Preface to Paradise Lost · Review

For me, the book is not a dry piece of academic criticism; it’s brilliant, passionate, and lucid. Lewis’s goal is simple: to “hinder hindrances” to the appreciation of Milton’s epic. He succeeds so thoroughly that reading the book helped clear my own modern prejudices away, allowing me to see the poem’s grand architecture for the first time.

The revolutionary argument is that we must judge Paradise Lost for what it is. As Lewis states in the opening chapter, “The first qualification for judging any piece of workmanship from a corkscrew to a cathedral is to know what it is.” We cannot read Milton’s epic as if it were a novel or a modern lyric poem and then complain when it fails to meet those expectations.

Lewis defines the epic form, distinguishing “Primary Epic” (like Homer’s) from “Secondary Epic” (like Milton’s). He then brilliantly defends the very things that used to turn me away from the poem.

① The Grand Style

Where others see “bogus” and “calculated” grandiosity, Lewis sees a necessary “ritual.” He argues that the “solemn,” “ceremonial” style, with its inversions and allusions, is essential to compensate for the loss of a live, public performance. It is an incantation designed to elevate the reader into the “festal” state of mind the form demands. This argument completely changed how I perceived the sound of the poem.

② The Unchanging Human Heart

Lewis dismantles the common critical practice of stripping away a work’s theology and philosophy to find some “relatable” modern values. He argues that it is a fool’s errand. Instead of “stripping the knight of his armour,” he insists, “you can try to put his armour on yourself.” We must, as readers, provisionally accept Milton’s worldview — his Augustinian theology, his belief in “hierarchy” — to understand the poem he actually wrote.

I had held the idea of Satan as a tragic hero, yet Lewis masterfully demolishes this. He argues that Satan’s character is, in fact, a study in “progressive degradation.” The supposed hero who begins with a “sense of injured merit” devolves “from hero to general, from general to politician, from politician to secret service agent, and thence to a thing that peers in at bedroom windows, and thence to a toad, and finally to a snake.” I found his argument that Satan is a “monomaniac” obsessed with himself, a creature who has chosen “Nonsense” as his good, completely persuasive — it’s an analysis that makes the poem far more terrifying and profound.

Lewis applies this same clarifying lens to Adam and Eve, arguing that they’re not “naif” savages but majestic, “severe,” and kingly beings, and to the poem’s theology, which he presents as the great, central, orthodox tradition of Christendom.

The Preface really stimulates us to go back to the poem itself. I immediately wanted to re-read Paradise Lost, feeling I was armed to actually understand it for the first time. It’s a rare work of criticism that is brilliant, persuasive, and, in its own way, timeless.

XI

Testing, Corruption and the Will

Macbeth and The Castle of Otranto · by Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

Macbeth chooses his corruption; Manfred inherits and defends his. One mind collapses inward, one castle is corrected from without.

Week 13 · Macbeth & The Castle of Otranto · Critical essay

In this reflection, I argue that Milton offers a testing-based model of morality. It’s a framework that clarifies the different ways Macbeth and Manfred collapse under pressure. Macbeth represents self-originating corruption, while Manfred represents inherited corruption. Both are forms of moral failure, but they arise through different disorders of the will.

When I first finished Macbeth, I experienced tragedy, ambition, and guilt; after reading Milton, I began to see the anatomy of moral malfunction. Here is my attempt to articulate this discovery.

Milton’s poem insists that rational beings must be tested, not for divine spectacle, but because virtue untested is never fully known. A creature’s freedom requires the possibility of misusing that freedom, and moral failure emerges when the will turns away from what is genuinely good. The two guiding ideas I learned from Milton are these:

① A moral test does not create corruption; it exposes it.

② When the will turns away from the good, the intellect becomes distorted.

This framework gave me a way to revisit Macbeth, and later, to understand The Castle of Otranto as a different but related drama of the same moral dynamics.

I · Macbeth — the Collapse of Chosen Corruption

When I read Shakespeare a month ago, I took Macbeth’s supernatural experiences — the witches, the dagger, Banquo’s ghost — as psychological symbols. But Milton helped me see them differently: not as illusions imposed on him, but as revelations of what is already inside him.

From the moment Macbeth reacts to the witches’ prophecy (“Why do I yield to that suggestion?”), we see not temptation but recognition. The suggestion resonates because the ambition is already in him. Under this framework:

① Macbeth knows murder is wrong.

② His will nevertheless inclines toward ambition.

③ That disordered will disfigures his ability to perceive reality (hallucinations, paranoia).

His visions are not supernatural intrusions but moral symptoms, evidence that evil has invaded the structure of his thinking. Macbeth’s tragedy is therefore inward: he falls not because he is cursed but because he freely chooses a path that destroys his rational and moral faculties. In this sense, Macbeth becomes a case study in self-generated corruption. He is ruined by the evil he authorizes within himself.

II · Manfred — the Collapse of Inherited Corruption

Walpole’s The Castle of Otranto presents a morally inverted version of this drama. Here, the protagonist’s crisis is not about committing a new sin but about defending an old one.

Manfred’s grandfather murdered the rightful ruler, Alfonso, and stole the castle. Manfred’s tyrannical behaviour — his cruelty, his attempted forced marriage, his desperation — is driven not by ambition to rise but by fear of falling. His moral test is therefore not “Will you commit evil?” but “Will you acknowledge the evil already done on your behalf?”

Unlike Macbeth’s internal visions, the supernatural in Otranto is external, undeniable and physically disruptive: a giant helmet crushing his son; a portrait stepping out of its frame; a statue bleeding; ancestral ghosts intervening. These phenomena function not as psychological metaphors but as cosmic corrections. The world itself refuses to legitimize an unjust lineage. Thus, Manfred’s fall is juridical rather than psychological: the universe overturns a corrupted inheritance he refuses to relinquish.

Both stories dramatize how the will, once deformed, disorders the entire person. Macbeth collapses from within; Manfred collapses from without. Milton gave me the vocabulary to articulate this difference. His understanding of testing — how trials reveal the moral structure of the soul — helped me see why these two characters fail in such different, yet equally catastrophic, ways.

III · Reflection

What struck me most as I moved from Macbeth to The Castle of Otranto is how differently each work imagines the collapse of a tyrant. Both Macbeth and Manfred attempt to secure or preserve power in defiance of a higher moral order, and both are ultimately destroyed by the forces they try to outmaneuver. Yet their paths diverge in ways that illustrate two distinct forms of moral failure.

Macbeth is ruined by the sin he actively chooses. He begins as a loyal warrior, praised for his courage and honored by his king. His downfall originates not in fate but in the inward tilt of his will. The prophecy does not coerce him; it awakens an ambition already present. Every supernatural encounter — whether the witches or Banquo’s ghost — reveals the internal corruption of a mind that has turned against the good it once served. Macbeth’s tragedy is the implosion of the self: he collapses from within, his reason distorted by the very desire he embraces.

Manfred, in contrast, is ruined by the sin he refuses to confront. His tyranny is not self-created but inherited. The original crime — the murder of the rightful prince Alfonso — was committed by an ancestor. Rather than acknowledging this injustice, Manfred clings to the illegitimate power passed down to him. In Walpole’s Gothic world, the supernatural functions as external judgment rather than psychological torment. The giant helmet, the bleeding statue, and the ghostly manifestations are not products of Manfred’s conscience but metaphysical corrections imposed by the moral structure of the universe. Where Macbeth’s hallucinations reveal internal disorder, Manfred’s hauntings enforce cosmic order.

Seen together, the two works form a study in complementary moral failures. Macbeth demonstrates how the will can distort the mind when it chooses evil freely; Manfred reveals how denial perpetuates inherited evil when the will refuses truth. One collapses inward, the other outward. The shape of their destruction differs, but the moral logic is the same: a disordered will cannot sustain the weight of its own desires.

Both Shakespeare and Walpole portray tyrants who fail the tests set before them, yet they fail in distinct ways. Macbeth is destroyed by the evil he chooses, while Manfred is destroyed by the evil he shields. Their stories expose two modes of moral collapse — one born of active ambition, the other of defensive inheritance. What unites these narratives is the recognition that moral integrity is not a passive condition but a continual alignment of the will with the good.

Tragedy teaches how corruption enters the soul; the Gothic teaches how corruption haunts the world. Together, they reveal that every moral test is a mirror in which the self finally sees itself — whether through the inward unraveling of Macbeth or the outward judgment that overtakes Manfred. In both cases, the human will stands at the center of the drama, determining whether one fails by choosing evil or by refusing to face it.

XII

Notes to the Reader on the Matter of Macbeth

by the Three Witches · Isabella Wu

❦

The Argument

The weird sisters answer the charge that they caused Macbeth’s fall — and hand the question back to the reader.

After Shakespeare’s Macbeth · The final essay

You think I am the reason Macbeth fell?

You sit there, centuries later, in your classrooms and library carrels, drawing neat lines in your notebooks: Witches → Prophecy → Ambition → Murder. Very elegant. Very simple. Maybe a bit too simple. Let me try to untangle this for you.

I am not the demon in Doctor Faustus, bargaining for a soul in exchange for twenty-four years of tricks and spectacle. I am not the Satan of Milton, arrayed in tattered grandeur, waging open war against the Highest Good. I am something quieter, and in a way, more unsettling: I am the disclosure of what is already there.

You call us "weird sisters." That is fair enough, though you rarely recall what wyrd once meant: not "strange," but "fate," "that which comes to pass." We are not the engineers of destiny; we are its commentators, its heralds, sometimes its dramatists. We do not manufacture Macbeth's desire. We simply give it a shape in language.

Let us start with a simple question, and I invite you to treat it with some philosophical seriousness: If Macbeth had never met us, would he still have been capable of murder?

This is not a trick. It is a modal question, the kind your analytic scholars might respect: in another possible world, one in which the fog is clear, the battlefield empty of us, the air free of prophecy. Could Macbeth have lived and died a loyal thane? Perhaps. But notice how quickly the "perhaps" becomes less certain once you actually read him closely.

Before we speak, Duncan has already rewarded him. Before we speak, Macbeth has already proven himself ruthless in battle. Before we speak, he has already shown himself capable of "unseaming" a man "from the nave to the chaps." He is, you might say, a man very comfortable with killing, provided it is covered by the cloak of honor. What he lacks is not the capacity for violence, but the conceptual bridge between legitimate killing in war and illegitimate killing for gain.

Our prophecy supplies that bridge — not as moral justification, but as imaginative permission. When we greet him as "Thane of Glamis," "Thane of Cawdor," and "king hereafter," we do not inject desire into him like a poison. Instead, we name a possibility he already finds attractive. Consider his reaction: he is startled, almost offended by how quickly his mind leaps ahead of the facts. He confesses later that the thought of murder had already occurred to him — "Whose horrid image doth unfix my hair" — before any concrete plan takes form. We did not produce that horrid image; we merely cast a bit of light on it.

In more careful terms: we are not the efficient cause of Macbeth's sin; we are the occasion that reveals the latent structure of his will.

You might think of human moral life as structured by counterfactuals of freedom. Those "if…then"s by which you test a person's character. If he were given the opportunity, then what would he do? In ordinary life you rarely see the full answer. People go to their graves never having encountered the particular combination of temptation, power, fear, and promise that would reveal what they could have done.

Our task, if you insist we have a task, is to bring such counterfactuals to actuality. We arrange conditions; we do not coerce choices. The difference is crucial. A coerced act is not truly of the will; it is more like a reflex under threat. Macbeth is not threatened by us. We offer nothing that binds him. No contract signed in blood, no infernal bargain is struck. We speak, vanish, and leave him with nothing more than a thought, and the dreadful freedom to decide what to do with it.

If you want to accuse someone of coercion, you will have to look elsewhere.

Notice also, that we do not say, "Kill Duncan." We do not hand Macbeth a dagger or whisper step-by-step instructions in his ear. We pronounce the future; we do not prescribe the means. If you want a rough analogy, think of a particularly shrewd psychologist or philosopher who understands you so well that she can predict the arc of your life — your likely choices, your patterns of failure, your rationalizations. If she quietly tells you what you are on track to become, is she thereby causing it?

You might answer: "Not necessarily, but she might strengthen the likelihood by making it salient." Good. Now we are speaking precisely. Our prophecies make latent possibilities salient, and then Macbeth does the rest. We do not manipulate the moral structure of his soul; we expose it.

This is why I object when you place me in the same conceptual file as Faustus's demon or Milton's Satan. Those figures are active corrupters. Faustus is wooed with promises tailored to his vanity and boredom: knowledge, pleasure, spectacle. Satan in Paradise Lost is a kind of metaphysical insurrectionist, bending his vast intellect toward the overthrow of divine order. Their operations are aggressive, interventionist, and almost missionary.

We are, by comparison, strangely austere. We do not offer Macbeth pleasure, nor protection, nor even assurance of success. We do not seduce him with arguments about justice or entitlement. We simply name him as something not yet actual, and watch what his will does with that information. If he were a different kind of man, he could have treated our greeting as a puzzle, a curiosity, perhaps even a temptation to be resisted and confessed. He does not. He takes it as an interpretive key to his own ambition.

You should also recall that the prophecy itself is partly self-fulfilling only because Macbeth makes it so. He kills Duncan to hasten what might have occurred, through some other succession, or perhaps never. He orchestrates Banquo's murder because he fears a prophecy about Banquo's descendants. In other words, he reorders the world in response to words that did not compel him.

To say "the witches made him do it" is not theologically lazy; it is psychologically false.

If you insist on assigning metaphysical categories, we are closer to epistemic tests than to demonic agents. We present him with information under conditions of moral risk: partial knowledge, suggestive language, and absence of clear instruction. This is actually quite similar to the way your own lives are structured. You almost never have total clarity. You act under conditions of incomplete data, mixed motives, and competing goods. It is precisely in such fog that the will shows its contour.

Macbeth, faced with ambiguity, chooses in favor of his darkest possibility.

Now, you might say: "But you knew he would." Perhaps. Perhaps not. Foreknowledge and causation, as your scholars have long noted, are logically independent. To know that someone will freely choose evil is not to make him choose it. We can anticipate his fall without forcing it. In that respect, we occupy a curious position at the edge of time: we see enough to speak truly, but not so much as to erase responsibility.

You may be tempted to protest that this makes us cold, even cruel. Why reveal a future that you know will be misused? Why not remain silent and spare him? But here you reveal a preference for ignorance over truth. You assume that not knowing one's capacity for evil is safer than knowing it. Macbeth's story suggests otherwise. His soul was not spotless before we met; it was merely unexamined.

There is, I will concede, a kind of mercy even in catastrophic revelation. A life can be wasted in quiet hypocrisy. Better, perhaps, to see clearly, even if what is revealed is ruinous. That is the risk built into human freedom: you are not merely creatures who act; you are creatures who can discover, too late, who you truly are.

Macbeth's tragedy is precisely this: he comes to recognize the horror of his own will, but only after he has set events in motion that cannot be undone. His laments toward the end — about "honour, love, obedience, troops of friends" that he shall not look to have — are not the words of a man deceived from outside. They are the words of a man who has finally seen the cost of his own decisions.

So no, I am not the evil creature in Doctor Faustus; I am not the tempter who offers signed terms and spectacular payoffs. Nor am I Milton's Satan, constructing a metaphysical rebellion against the structure of goodness itself. I occupy a subtler, and I think more philosophically instructive, role. I am the voice that turns a possible self into a thinkable self.

I do not drag Macbeth downward. I hold up a mirror angled just so, and he recognizes, with a mixture of terror and fascination, the man he could become. He chooses to step into that reflection. After that, he scarcely needs us at all. His imagination, once set loose, performs the rest.

You may, if you wish, continue to comfort yourselves with the story that evil comes from elsewhere: from witches, demons, systems, histories — any source that lets individual wills sit in the back row of the courtroom. But texts like Macbeth keep insisting, in their own rough and poetic way, that the decisive theatre of moral evil is inward. External voices, whether ours, or the witches in your own lives, cannot cause what is not already, in some embryonic way, desired.

So next time you read Macbeth and feel the impulse to blame us, pause. Ask instead: What does his response to the prophecy reveal about the structure of his soul? Notice when he wavers, when he rationalizes, when he invokes destiny as a cover for his own choice. Trace how quickly he moves from startled listener to calculating murderer. Then ask the more uncomfortable question: What would I have done, hearing such a greeting on a lonely heath after victory and praise?

My point, if I must put it plainly, is this: we are not the origin of Macbeth's corruption. We are the occasion on which that corruption becomes unmistakably visible.

And perhaps that is why, despite your efforts to domesticate or dismiss us, we remain on your stage, in your classrooms, and now in your own essays. We are not there to terrify you with exotic evil. We are there to suggest that the real terror may lie much closer to home: in the quiet, unforced, but all too eager assent of the human will to the darkest of its own possibilities.

Artist Statement

In this piece, I aimed to reimagine one of Shakespeare's most misunderstood figures: the witches. Popular readings of Macbeth often cast the witches as the instigators of evil, reducing them to convenient villains who trigger the protagonist's fall. My artistic goal was to dismantle that assumption by giving the witch a reflective, philosophical voice — the kind that is capable of analyzing human agency with precision and moral seriousness.

Stylistically, I shaped the narrative around the structure of a rational argument. Rather than portraying the witch as chaotic, supernatural, or malicious, I constructed her as an analytic observer of human freedom. This choice allowed me to position her as a commentator on Macbeth's interior life, rather than a participant in his corruption. Her tone is measured, careful, and intellectually rigorous, echoing the discursive clarity of moral philosophers who are more interested in diagnosing the will than in dramatizing it.

I also intentionally positioned the witch against other literary tempters, such as Mephistopheles in Doctor Faustus and Milton's Satan. By contrasting her with figures who actively corrupt, I clarified the conceptual distinction central to the piece: the witches in Macbeth do not manipulate Macbeth's moral structure, they reveal it. This aligns with my broader creative aim of exploring how narratives often displace responsibility outward, obscuring the inward sources of moral failure.

This piece also draws inspiration from Ta-Nehisi Coates, particularly the reflective, declarative prose style he uses in Between the World and Me. I was drawn to his method of writing in a mode that is both analytical and deeply personal, as though thinking and confessing at the same time. His voice often feels like someone sorting through the truth in real time, refusing easy answers or comfortable illusions. That quality informed the tone I wanted for the witch's voice.

Using a contemporary reflective voice makes the witch feel less mythic, and more unsettlingly human, as if her account belongs as much in a modern essay as on a medieval heath. This approach allowed me to explore Macbeth's moral collapse with a tone that is meditative rather than mystical, analytic rather than accusatory. Ultimately, I aim to shift the reader's attention from external blame to internal agency. By inhabiting the witch's perspective, I invite the audience to reconsider where corruption truly begins, and what it means to confront the moral self honestly.
